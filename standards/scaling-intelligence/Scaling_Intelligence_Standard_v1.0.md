# Scaling Intelligence Standard

**Version:** 1.0
**Status:** Frozen governing reference for the current cycle
**Discipline:** Scaling Intelligence — one of three core disciplines of the Scaling Intelligence Operating System
**Scope:** Governs the production, review, and use of all Scaling Intelligence Assessments (SIAs)

---

## 1. Purpose and scope

### 1.1 What this standard governs

This is the governing standard for the **Scaling Intelligence** discipline. It defines what a Scaling Intelligence Assessment (SIA) is, the evidence and reasoning it must meet, and how it is reviewed. It is deliberately concise. Templates, checklists, evidence schemas, workflows, and worked examples are **referenced, not reproduced here** (§11).

The role of Scaling Intelligence is to **transform Scaling Knowledge into decision-ready intelligence** that directly supports strategy, coaching, investment decisions, and portfolio management. The Scaling Librarian preserves what is known; the Scaling Intelligence discipline determines what it means for a decision.

### 1.2 What an SIA is

An SIA is a **decision-support product that converts fragmented scaling evidence into an allocated, uncertainty-honest judgment about whether and how a Solution Track should advance.**

It supports five decisions:

- **Stage-gating** — advance, hold, adapt, or stop
- **Coaching** — where a team should focus limited attention next
- **Portfolio management** — how a Track compares to others competing for the same resources
- **Investment** — whether the scaling case is fundable and what the money buys
- **Adaptive learning** — what was believed, what happened, what is now known

### 1.3 What an SIA is not

An SIA is not a methodology output, not a summary of documents, and not an advocacy document. It does not assume an innovation should scale; **"stop" is a legitimate and valuable conclusion.** It does not reproduce IPSR, Scaling Scan, Innovation Packaging, or any other framework — it learns from them and treats them as evidence inputs.

---

## 2. Governing principle

Existing methodologies answer *"what did we assess?"* Decision-makers ask four questions methodologies rarely answer cleanly. Every SIA exists to answer these four:

1. **Will this scale, and what must be true for it to scale?** (the causal claim)
2. **What is the single thing most likely to stop it?** (the binding constraint)
3. **How sure are we, and what would change our mind?** (confidence and its fragility)
4. **Who must do what next, and whose job is it?** (allocated action)

If an SIA does not answer these four, it is not decision-ready, regardless of length or polish.

---

## 3. Primary users

An SIA has one evidence base and one judgment, rendered through role-specific views. The confidence statement (§8) is **identical across all views** — different audiences may see different detail, never different confidence.

| User | Decision they make | What they need most |
|---|---|---|
| Solution Track team | What do we do next? | Binding constraint; adaptive action plan; honest confidence |
| AoW2 (Scaling function) | Coach, support, stage-gate | Dependency diagnosis; what AoW2 can and cannot do; gate readiness |
| Portfolio / program leadership | Allocate resources across Tracks | Comparable confidence and readiness signals |
| Donors / investors | Fund or not; what money buys | The causal case, its weakest link, cost to de-risk it |
| Scaling partners | Commit capacity or not | Demand evidence; role clarity; what is expected |
| Governments / policy actors | Institutionalize or enable | Enabling-environment and policy dependencies and their status |

## 4. Required structure

Every SIA follows this ten-section structure, in decision-priority order (the conclusion leads; it is never buried). The canonical layout and inline authoring prompts live in the **SIA Assessment Template** (§11).

1. **Decision Frame** — the decision, the gate or question, the named owner. If this is unclear, the SIA is not yet worth producing.
2. **Headline Judgment** — recommended decision (advance / hold / adapt / stop), overall confidence, the single binding constraint. Written last, placed first, readable in 60 seconds.
3. **Scaling Ambition & Causal Logic** — what is scaled, for whom, by whom, to what magnitude, and the explicit chain of *what must be true*. This is the spine everything else tests.
4. **Evidence Synthesis** — organized by capability, not by methodology or document; every claim tagged (§5).
5. **Weakest-Link Diagnosis** — the binding constraint, the runners-up, the de-risking cost (§7).
6. **Uncertainty & Critical Assumptions** — the assumptions register and what fails if each is false (§6).
7. **Responsible Scaling & Inclusion** — who benefits, who is excluded, who bears risk, trade-offs, and any stop condition. A full section, not a footnote — it can itself be the binding constraint.
8. **Stage-Gate Reading** — met / asserted / missing against the gate; the gate recommendation (§9).
9. **Allocated Recommendations** — actions split by owner, each with a decision rule (§10).
10. **Confidence & Decision-Readiness Statement** — overall confidence, its basis and fragility, and the decision-ready check (§12).

## 5. Evidence standards

Every substantive claim carries three tags. The full schema lives in **SIA Evidence Standards** (§11); the governing rules are:

**Epistemic status:** Observed · Interpretation · Hypothesis · Unknown.

**Evidence type (ranked, because type governs weight):**

1. Independently validated outcome evidence
2. Monitoring / MEL data
3. Comparative or portfolio evidence
4. Field observation
5. Expert judgment / coaching assessment
6. Participant self-assessment (e.g. workshop-scored readiness)
7. Conceptual / methodological design claim

**Provenance:** the specific Knowledge Card, Evidence Record, dataset, or named source.

Three hard rules govern all evidence use:

- **No provenance, no claim.** Filenames, folder placement, and document titles are not evidence.
- **Method-output ≠ outcome.** A high method score is type-6 self-assessment, not evidence that scaling occurs. This is the most common error the SIA exists to prevent.
- **Independence is tracked.** Multiple documents from one methodology family are one source family, not independent corroboration.

Evidence type governs **how much weight a claim carries and how confidence is scored** — it does **not** set a minimum bar to produce an SIA. The bar rises with the gate (§12), not with the assessment.

---

## 6. Representation of uncertainty

Uncertainty is a first-class section, never a hedging phrase in prose. Three required devices:

- **Critical Assumptions Register** — each load-bearing assumption listed with why it matters, its evidence status, and *what happens to the scaling case if it is false*. Assumptions whose failure collapses the case are flagged **load-bearing**.
- **Known-unknown / unknown-risk split** — separate "we know we don't know X" (a research task) from "this could fail in ways we haven't mapped" (a monitoring task).
- **Decision-sensitivity note** — state whether resolving the biggest uncertainty would change the recommended decision. If yes, it becomes the top action. If no, the Track can advance despite not knowing everything.

Uncertainty is represented as **consequence, not level**: not "how uncertain are we" but "does the uncertainty change the decision."

---

## 7. Weakest-link analysis

Weakest-link reasoning is the analytical core of every SIA and is grounded in systems-based scaling strategy.

The chain is the causal logic of §4.3. Scaling requires a sequence of things to be true — demand, delivery reach, partner incentive, sustaining finance, a permitting enabling environment. **The scaling case is only as strong as its weakest link.**

Required method:

1. Lay out the links from the causal logic, mapped to capabilities.
2. Assess each link's strength *and* its evidence status — a link may be weak, or it may be unknown; these are different problems.
3. Identify the **binding constraint**: the single link whose failure most threatens scaling and is not currently being resolved. Distinguish it from a generic bottleneck list.
4. State the **de-risking cost**: what it would take — time, money, whose action — to strengthen it, because that is what a decision-maker actually buys.

The binding constraint is frequently **actor incentive**, not technical readiness. It is a **claim** and must be evidenced; where it cannot be, the honest output is *"the weakest link is unknown — finding it is the priority."*

---

## 8. Communication of confidence

One shared scale, applied to the headline judgment and each major claim:

**Well supported · Moderately supported · Emerging · Speculative · Insufficient evidence.**

Confidence is always communicated as a **triple, never a bare label:**

> **[Level] · because [basis] · and it would change if [what would move it].**

Two firm rules:

- **Confidence is separate from favorability.** A confident "stop" is as valuable as a confident "advance." High confidence is not good news.
- **The confidence statement is identical across all role views.** Color may indicate the recommended *decision* in an executive view; confidence is **always** shown as the worded triple, never as a color.

---

## 9. Stage-gating

An SIA reads evidence **against the gate**, using the three-stage model: Proof of Concept → Transition to Scale → Scaling.

For the relevant gate the SIA states which gate applies, the exit criteria for the current stage and entry criteria for the next, what is met (Observed) / asserted (Interpretation, Hypothesis) / missing (Unknown), and a gate recommendation of advance / hold / adapt / stop with the binding constraint as the pivot.

The **evidence bar rises with the gate** (§12). An SIA **never** issues a bare pass/fail; it issues a decision with its confidence and binding constraint attached, so a "hold" reads as *"here is the one thing to resolve to advance,"* not *"rejected."* Coaching and gating are the same act done well.

---

## 10. Allocation of recommendations

Every recommendation names an **owner**, a **decision rule**, and an **evidence-it-worked signal**. Unowned recommendations are prohibited. Owner classes are governed by S4I Area-of-Work boundaries:

| Owner | May be asked to | Boundary |
|---|---|---|
| Solution Track team | Gather evidence, test assumptions, adapt the innovation/package, engage users | Owns the innovation and its evidence |
| AoW2 (Scaling) | Coach, diagnose, support strategy, facilitate gating, broker connections | **May diagnose dependencies on enabling environment and finance; may not claim to deliver AoW3/AoW4 functions** |
| Other AoWs | Deliver functions outside AoW2's remit | Named as a dependency and handoff, not commanded |
| External partners | Delivery, market linkage, finance, policy, institutionalization | Named with the *incentive* that would make them act; an unincented action is not a plan |

The AoW2 boundary is preserved **in the grammar of the recommendation**: a diagnosed finance constraint is written as a dependency AoW2 must surface and route, never as a function AoW2 will deliver.

---

## 11. Responsible scaling

Responsible scaling is a required section, not a compliance addendum. Every SIA states who benefits, who is excluded, who bears risk, and what environmental or social trade-offs may emerge. Responsible-scaling and inclusion concerns can themselves constitute a **binding constraint or a stop condition**, and are weighted accordingly in the headline judgment.

---

## 12. Decision-readiness

An SIA is **decision-ready** only when it clears five gates. Below this bar it is an **early-stage working assessment** — useful for coaching, not valid for stage-gating or investment.

1. **Decision frame present** — a specific decision with a named owner.
2. **Causal logic explicit** — the "what must be true" chain is drawn.
3. **Binding constraint identified or explicitly declared unknown.**
4. **Load-bearing assumptions registered with evidence status** — no silent assumptions in the causal spine.
5. **Confidence stated with basis and fragility** — the §8 triple for the headline judgment.

These five gates concern **reasoning quality** and are always required. The **evidence-type** bar is separate and rises with the gate:

| Transition | Minimum credible evidence for a *decision-ready* SIA |
|---|---|
| PoC → Transition to Scale | Credible causal logic + strong type-4/5/6 evidence + honest confidence |
| Transition → Scaling | Type-1/2 outcome evidence on the binding-constraint link + validated demand + a delivery/finance/incentive answer |

> **Open calibration point.** The strictness of the Transition → Scaling outcome-evidence threshold is provisionally set and is to be calibrated against the first real Scaling-gate SIA rather than settled in the abstract, consistent with the principle that field evidence beats workshop opinion. Tracked in the open-questions log (§14).

**Production floor:** if the decision cannot be stated (§4.1) and the causal logic cannot be drawn (§4.3), do not produce an SIA — produce a scoping note instead. Weak evidence does not block production; it sets the ceiling on confidence and the gate the SIA can support.

---

## 13. Review and quality assurance

Every SIA passes a two-stage, human-in-the-loop review before it is treated as decision-ready. The pass/fail items live in the **SIA Review Checklist** (§11).

- **Stage 1 — Author self-check** against the checklist: every claim tagged with status, type, and provenance; no method-output mislabeled as outcome; source-family independence visible; weakest link evidenced or explicitly Unknown; every recommendation owned with a decision rule; AoW2 boundary respected; confidence a triple, separate from favorability; the five decision-readiness gates marked.
- **Stage 2 — Two-role review.** Evidence integrity is reviewed for support, honest confidence, and preserved tensions; decision usefulness is reviewed for a real binding constraint, actionable and correctly owned recommendations, and clarity of next action. **Where the two reviews disagree, the disagreement is surfaced in the SIA, not resolved away.**

**Status field.** Every SIA carries a status: `DRAFT` → `REVIEWED` → `REPOSITORY`. Only a **named human reviewer** may move an SIA to `REPOSITORY`. Until then, an SIA is a working analysis, not repository truth. The repository is the authoritative source of truth.

## 14. Governance of this standard

This standard is **frozen for the current cycle**. Refinements are gathered as template patches and open-question log entries, not by editing the standard mid-cycle. Open questions carried into use:

- the Transition → Scaling evidence threshold (§12);
- the risk of contradictory confidence signals across layered role views (§3);
- the rule for flagging capabilities that rest on a single methodology (§5).

These resolve through evidence from real SIAs, not through further architecture.

## 15. Referenced artifacts

This standard governs; it does not duplicate. The following implementation artifacts are maintained separately and are authoritative for their own scope:

- **SIA Assessment Template** — the fillable ten-section skeleton with inline authoring prompts and enforced evidence tags (§4, §5).
- **SIA Evidence Standards** — the full three-tag schema, evidence-type definitions, and the decision-ready bar (§5, §12).
- **SIA Review Checklist** — the Stage-1 and Stage-2 pass/fail QA items (§13).
- **SIA Worked Example** — one completed, reviewed SIA on a real Solution Track, produced through use (§13).
- **SIA Canonical Standard v1.0 (design record)** — the full design rationale, including the Analyst/Coach debates behind each rule, retained for provenance.

Methodology inputs (IPSR, Scaling Scan, GenderUp, PAASS, LINK, AICCRA Scaling Playbook, Scaling Readiness, and systems-based scaling strategy) are treated as evidence sources under §5, never as the assessment itself.
